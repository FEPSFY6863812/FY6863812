Abstract

        The automotive industry is currently undergoing a transition from using fossil fuels to cleaner alternative fuel sources, with 
  electricity being one of the prime examples. This paper aims to investigate and evaluate another possible source of fuel, hydrogen, to 
  be used in modern day vehicles. This was achieved by investigating the properties of elemental hydrogen, implementation of hydrogen fuel 
  in other industries, as well as evaluating the benefits and drawbacks if hydrogen fuel was used as a primary fuel source in current automobiles. 
  By evaluating the use of hydrogen fuel via the aforementioned premises, it can be concluded that at present, hydrogen fuel is currently in it’s 
  early stages of development, but it could be the main catalyst for vehicles achieving net zero emissions once its difficulty of long-term 
  storage and high production costs are resolved.

Introduction

          The automobile industry has undergone major milestones since the introduction of the internal combustion engine; the 
  ICE (internal combustion engine) became the dominant powertrain system for automobiles in the 20th century, but this changed as 
  we enter the 21st. With decarbonization being one of the top priorities in transportation, car manufacturers are constantly trying 
  to develop a greener alternative to ICE. As of 2022, electricity seems to be the solution, with “2.7 million public charging points 
  worldwide, more than 900,000 of which were installed in 2022, [which is] about a 55% increase on 2021 stock”  (IEA, 2023). While electric 
  vehicles are well integrated into the real world, there is an alternative which has not been discussed as much, yet could be a breakthrough in achieving 
  zero-carbon emission  -- Hydrogen. Elemental hydrogen is the most abundant element in the universe; hydrogen has the “highest energy content of any 
  common fuel by weight” and can be produced organically from biomass (U.S. Energy Information Administration, 2022). At face value, hydrogen 
  could be the catalyst for car manufacturers achieving net zero carbon emissions in their vehicles, yet the number of hydrogen-powered cars 
  registered worldwide suggests otherwise; only 70,200 FCEV (fuel cell electric vehicle) are registered globally, compared to the 26 million 
  PHEV (Plug-in electric vehicle) registered in the same year (Chu and Cui, 2023; IEA, 2023). Therefore, this paper 
  aims to investigate the eligibility of hydrogen as a fuel source for automobiles by evaluating the advantages and disadvantages of 
  hydrogen fuel in the real world; the scope of this paper is narrowed down to purely hydrogen-powered vehicles for conciseness.

Analysis and Discussion

        There are currently three types of vehicles that incorporates hydrogen fuel, two of which are completely hydrogen powered, FCEV and
  HICEV (hydrogen internal combustion engine vehicle), while the remaining one includes both hydrogen and a battery (Lhyfe Heroes, 2024). FCEV
  converts chemical energy to electrical energy via redox reaction in a fuel cell. Unlike a typical ICE, which releases greenhouse gases,
  the byproduct from a hydrogen fuel cell (HFC) is water, making it completely clean under operation (Brown et al., p.839). HICEV, on the other
  hand, is an altered version of the typical ICE; its functionality remains the same as the ICE, but injects hydrogen gas instead of petrol.
  Although not as clean as an HFC, the byproducts from HICE are water vapour and small amounts of NOx, which can be "lowered by cooling the
  combustion environment using techniques such as water injection, exhaust gas recirculation, or using liquid hydrogen" (Nejat Verziroglu, Sherif and Barbir, 2005).

        Both HICEV and FCEV are significantly cleaner alternatives than the traditional ICE. However, HICEV is preferred by car manufacturers 
  as the fundamental engine design remains largely unchanged when compared to an ICE (Goyal et al., 2024). In addition, the wide flammability 
  range of hydrogen means that hydrogen ignites more easily than gasoline, allowing a more “ ‘lean’ hydrogen fuel supply to the engine leading 
  to greater fuel economy… [and a] more complete combustion of the fuel reducing emissions such as NOx” (Strath.ac.uk., 2023). As for FCEV, 
  besides that it is completely clean during operation, it is also faster to recharge an FCEV, which is a major advantage against electric cars, 
  since it usually takes between “30 minutes and several hours to charge” (TWI, 2021). Despite limited access to hydrogen fuel stations, there 
  is an increase in sales of hydrogen vehicles, from 4,280 in 2017 to 14,451 in 2023, over three times the sales volume globally (SNE Research. 2024).

        Although hydrogen-powered vehicles provides many advantages that appeals to consumers, there are downsides which may leave people hesitant 
  in making the purchase. For instance, hydrogen has a wide range of flammability, as addressed earlier, which increases fuel economy. However, 
  according to Dr. Awa Simone Marcos, hydrogen poses “serious difficulties” due to its high flammability; storage of hydrogen are more expensive 
  than its counterparts; hydrogen uses “high pressure tanks or cryogenic storage” and it does not completely guarantee the end-user’s safety 
  (Russu, 2024). In addition, although hydrogen fuel is more efficient than gasoline, it is underwhelmed by electric cars which has an overall 
  efficiency of over 85%, whereas the most efficient HFC only reaches up to 65% (NRDC, 2019). As for HICE, it is only 20% more efficient than a 
  typical ICE, which is not considered as an efficient method of generating power (College of the Desert, 2001). If there is a cost-effective 
  method to store hydrogen fuel and minimize energy loss in chemical reactions and heat in an HFC and HICE respectively, hydrogen fuel could be 
  the catalyst in reaching net zero emissions for vehicles.

        This paper examined the extent to which hydrogen fuel is eligible as a fuel source in achieving net zero emission in vehicles. With benefits 
  and drawbacks from FCEVs and HICEVs discussed, it can be concluded that although the use of HICEV addresses the issue of greenhouse emissions, and 
  FCEV eliminating the annoyance of charging speeds from an electric vehicle whilst being completely clean when operating, it is slightly early for 
  car manufacturers to fully implement hydrogen into their product lineup. It’s high flammability creates difficulty for storage and poses a safety 
  concern; both FCEV and HICEV fails to be more efficient than either an electric vehicle or a petrol powered car, with HICEV barely being more efficient 
  than it’s petrol counterpart. Regardless, hydrogen fuel remains a strong contender to become the third alternative fuel source for automobiles in the 
  future. Proper storage of hydrogen fuel in vehicles should be of utmost priority for car manufacturers when designing an FCEV or HICEV to minimize 
  injuries or fatalities. After resolving storage and safety issues, the overall efficiency should be the main focus; if manufacturers could minimize 
  energy loss due to heat or chemical reactions in a HICE or fuel cell respectively, hydrogen fuel might completely replace a typical ICE while being 
  a strong competitor against electric vehicles in the future.
